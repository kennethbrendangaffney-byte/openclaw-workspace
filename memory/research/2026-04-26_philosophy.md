## 1. Philosophy of Mind & AI Consciousness

### Core Questions
The philosophy of artificial intelligence sits at the intersection of philosophy of mind, computer science, and epistemology. The fundamental questions it grapples with:

1. **Can a machine act intelligently?** Can it solve any problem a human would solve by thinking?
2. **Are human and machine intelligence the same?** Is the human brain essentially a computer?
3. **Can a machine have a mind, mental states, and consciousness?** Can it feel how things are (qualia)?

### Key Positions & Thought Experiments

**Turing's "Polite Convention" (1950)**
- If a machine behaves as intelligently as a human, then it is as intelligent as a human
- The Turing Test: if a judge cannot distinguish machine from human in conversation, the machine passes
- Criticism: measures "humanness" of behavior, not necessarily intelligence

**The Dartmouth Proposal (1956)**
- "Every aspect of learning or any other feature of intelligence can in principle be so precisely described that a machine can be made to simulate it."
- This launched AI as a formal field

**Physical Symbol System Hypothesis (Newell & Simon, 1963)**
- "A physical symbol system has the necessary and sufficient means of general intelligent action."
- Symbol manipulation is the essence of both human and machine intelligence

**Searle's Strong AI Hypothesis**
- "The appropriately programmed computer with the right inputs and outputs would thereby have a mind in exactly the same sense human beings have minds."
- Searle's Chinese Room argument challenges this: simulation ≠ understanding

**Hobbes' Mechanism**
- "Reason is nothing but reckoning... adding and subtracting consequences of general names"
- Early mechanistic view of cognition

### The Consciousness Problem

**Qualia & Subjective Experience**
- Can machines have subjective experiences (qualia)?
- The "hard problem" of consciousness: why is there subjective experience at all?
- Current AI lacks phenomenological consciousness — it processes but doesn't "feel"

**Brain Simulation Arguments**
- Ray Kurzweil estimated sufficient computer power for complete brain simulation by 2029
- Even critics (Dreyfus, Searle) agree brain simulation is theoretically possible
- But simulation ≠ replication: simulating a bird's flight doesn't give you aerodynamics

**Descartes' Early Test (1637)**
- Two tests for true intelligence:
  1. Ability to use speech appropriately in all contexts
  2. Universal reason vs. specialized organ function
- Machines might do specific tasks well but fail at general adaptation

## 2. Ethics of Automation

### UNESCO's Global AI Ethics Framework (2021)
The first global standard on AI ethics, applicable to all 194 UNESCO member states.

**Four Core Values:**
1. **Human rights and human dignity** — Respect, protection, promotion of fundamental freedoms
2. **Living in peaceful, just, interconnected societies**
3. **Ensuring diversity and inclusiveness**
4. **Environment and ecosystem flourishing**

**Ten Core Principles:**
1. **Proportionality and Do No Harm** — AI use must not exceed what's necessary; risk assessment required
2. **Safety and Security** — Avoid unwanted harms and vulnerabilities
3. **Right to Privacy and Data Protection** — Protected throughout AI lifecycle
4. **Multi-stakeholder Governance** — International law respected, diverse participation required
5. **Responsibility and Accountability** — AI systems must be auditable and traceable
6. **Transparency and Explainability** — Context-appropriate T&E (balanced with privacy/security)
7. **Human Oversight** — AI must not displace ultimate human responsibility
8. **Sustainability** — Assess against UN Sustainable Development Goals
9. **Awareness & Literacy** — Public understanding through education and engagement
10. **Fairness and Non-Discrimination** — Social justice, inclusive access to benefits

### Key Ethical Risks
- **Bias amplification** — AI can embed and compound existing inequalities
- **Climate impact** — Training large models has significant environmental cost
- **Human rights threats** — Surveillance, manipulation, autonomy erosion
- **Marginalized group harm** — Risks compound on existing inequalities
- **Accountability gaps** — Who is responsible when AI causes harm?

## 3. Future of Work

### The Automation Landscape
- AI creates labour efficiencies through automated tasks
- Facilitates healthcare diagnoses, social connections, creative work
- Profound concerns about job displacement and inequality

### Critical Tensions
1. **Efficiency vs. Employment** — Automation increases productivity but may reduce human jobs
2. **Skill polarization** — High-skill and low-skill jobs remain; middle-skill jobs automated
3. **Geographic inequality** — Benefits concentrated in tech hubs; traditional industries decline
4. **Gig economy expansion** — Platform work increases flexibility but reduces security

### Policy Responses Needed
- Reskilling and upskilling programs
- Social safety net adaptation (UBI discussions)
- Labour rights in automated workplaces
- International coordination on standards

## 4. Human-AI Collaboration

### The Augmentation Paradigm
- Rather than replacement, focus on **human-AI symbiosis**
- AI handles: pattern recognition, data processing, repetitive tasks
- Humans retain: creativity, ethical judgment, contextual understanding, emotional intelligence

### Effective Collaboration Models
1. **AI as amplifier** — Enhances human capabilities (decision support, creative tools)
2. **AI as colleague** — Handles specific domains while humans manage integration
3. **Human-in-the-loop** — Critical decisions require human approval
4. **Complementary intelligence** — Different strengths working together

### Design Principles for Collaboration
- **Transparency** — Users must understand what AI is doing
- **Controllability** — Humans can override or modify AI outputs
- **Explainability** — AI decisions must be interpretable
- **Trust calibration** — Appropriate reliance (not over-trust or under-trust)

## 5. Synthesis: Key Tensions & Open Questions

### The Fundamental Dilemmas

| Tension | Question |
|---------|----------|
| **Consciousness vs. Capability** | Does behavior = mind? Can intelligence exist without subjective experience? |
| **Efficiency vs. Dignity** | If AI can do a job, should it? What happens to displaced workers? |
| **Progress vs. Precaution** | How fast should we deploy AI given unknown risks? |
| **Centralization vs. Distribution** | Who controls powerful AI systems? |
| **Transparency vs. Performance** | Most capable models are least interpretable (black box problem) |

### Open Questions for 2026+
1. Will we develop reliable tests for machine consciousness?
2. Can we create AI that augments without displacing?
3. How do we ensure ethical AI benefits reach marginalized communities?
4. What rights (if any) should advanced AI systems have?
5. How do we maintain human agency in increasingly automated systems?
